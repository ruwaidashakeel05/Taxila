Taxila

The City of Cut Stone — a 2,600-year-old crossroads of Vedic, Greek, Buddhist and Kushan civilisation, and the site UNESCO just warned Pakistan about.

📍 35 km NW of Islamabad, Punjab 🏛️ UNESCO World Heritage Site, 1980 ⚠️ Under active UNESCO review, July 2026

Why Taxila

A university before the word existed

Long before Oxford or Nalanda, Taxila — known in Sanskrit as Takshashila, "the City of Cut Stone" — was drawing scholars, monks and traders from three continents. It sat where the Royal Highway from eastern India met roads from western Asia and from Kashmir/Central Asia, which is exactly why it absorbed so many civilisations: Achaemenid Persian, Greek, Mauryan, Scythian, Parthian, Kushan — until Hunnic invasion brought it down in the 5th century CE.

It isn't one ruin — it's an 18-site archaeological region spanning roughly a 10 km radius, layering four separate cities (Saraikala, Bhir Mound, Sirkap, Sirsukh) one on top of the other like sediment, plus the Buddhist monastery-and-stupa complexes at Dharmarajika, Jaulian, and Mohra Moradu.

Timeline

From ancient crossroads to the bulldozer of 2026

  • c. 1000 BCE

    First major settlement at Hathial mound; early trade links with the wider Gandhara region.

  • 6th c. BCE

    Gandhara, with Taxila as a key city, is absorbed into the Achaemenid Persian Empire.

  • 327 BCE

    Alexander the Great reaches Taxila on his campaign into the subcontinent.

  • 3rd c. BCE

    Mauryan rule; the city becomes a renowned centre of learning, traditionally linked to scholars such as Chanakya.

  • 2nd c. BCE – 1st c. CE

    Indo-Greek city of Sirkap laid out on a Hellenistic grid — home to the famous Double-Headed Eagle Stupa.

  • 1st–2nd c. CE

    Kushan Empire; Sirsukh founded by Kanishka. Golden age of Gandharan Buddhist art at Jaulian, Mohra Moradu and Dharmarajika.

  • 5th c. CE

    Hun invasions destroy the city; it fades from history for over a thousand years.

  • 1863–1873

    Sir Alexander Cunningham rediscovers and identifies the ruins for the Archaeological Survey of India.

  • 1913–1934

    Sir John Marshall's systematic excavations reveal Sirkap, Sirsukh, and the monastery complexes.

  • 1980

    Taxila inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List.

  • March 2026

    A visitor alerts Pakistan's Permanent Delegate to UNESCO to fresh cement masonry replacing original stonework at two sites.

  • July 2026

    UNESCO formally warns Pakistan to reverse the work or risk Taxila being placed on the List of World Heritage in Danger.

Why it matters right now

This isn't a history problem. It's happening this month.

In March 2026, photos submitted to UNESCO showed original, irregular ancient stonework at Mohra Moradu (a Kushan-era Buddhist monastery) and Sirkap (the Indo-Greek city layer) replaced with fresh, uniform modern masonry and cement — in places rebuilt taller than the originals. UNESCO called these "unnecessary interventions" that damage authenticity, and referenced its earlier delisting of a World Heritage Site in Germany for the same reason.

The stakes

Punjab's Archaeology Department disputes the framing, calling the work structural stabilisation rather than rebuilding — but the underlying tension is what this whole project is about: what does it actually mean to "preserve" a place like this, and who decides? A site that survived a thousand years of empires can still lose its status over a decade of well-intentioned concrete.

Turn your own photo into a point cloud

This uses the same monocular depth heuristic developed for our team's Jaulian monastery reconstruction: it reads brightness to separate sky/background from foreground, reads edge strength from image gradients, and combines both with vertical position to guess a rough depth for every pixel. It is not real photogrammetry or multi-view Structure-from-Motion — our team tested that on the curated site photos first and it wasn't viable, so this is a deliberately single-image heuristic. Treat the result as illustrative, not measured geometry. It works best on photos with a brighter sky/background and a darker, textured foreground subject.

Beyond Taxila

The Gandhara Learning Route

Taxila is the anchor, not the whole story. Gandharan civilisation ran along a corridor from Taxila through Peshawar into the Mardan hilltops and up to the Swat Valley, where its sculpture reached its peak. This is the 3-day study circuit we used to place Taxila in context.

Day 1 — Taxila, the layered city

Taxila Museum → Sirkap (Indo-Greek grid city) → Jaulian monastery, the site currently at the centre of the UNESCO conservation dispute.

Day 2 — North to Mardan

Peshawar Museum's Gandharan sculpture hall → Takht-i-Bahi's hilltop monastic cells → the quieter Jamal Garhi ruins.

Day 3 — Swat, where the art peaked

Swat Museum → Butkara I, an Ashokan-era stupa damaged under militant control and since restored — a second live preservation case study.

Who this is for

Audience & demographic context

Local day-trippers

Families and students from Islamabad/Rawalpindi — Taxila is a ~45-minute drive via the Grand Trunk Road, Pakistan's most accessible major heritage site.

Regional & diaspora Buddhists

Pilgrims from Sri Lanka, Southeast Asia, Korea and Japan, for whom Gandharan Taxila is foundational to the spread of Buddhist art and doctrine.

Heritage & conservation students

The current UNESCO dispute makes Taxila a live case study in conservation ethics — exactly the audience this page and presentation are aimed at.
