Taxila

The City of Cut Stone — a 2,600-year-old crossroads of Vedic, Greek, Buddhist and Kushan civilisation, and the site UNESCO just warned Pakistan about.

📍 35 km NW of Islamabad, Punjab 🏛️ UNESCO World Heritage Site, 1980 ⚠️ Under active UNESCO review, July 2026

Why Taxila

A university before the word existed

Long before Oxford or Nalanda, Taxila — known in Sanskrit as Takshashila, "the City of Cut Stone" — was drawing scholars, monks and traders from three continents. It sat at the meeting point of the Royal Highway from eastern India, a route from western Asia, and a road from Kashmir and Central Asia, which is exactly why it absorbed so many civilisations: Achaemenid Persian, Greek, Mauryan, Scythian, Parthian, Kushan, and finally Hunnic invasion brought it down in the 5th century CE.

It isn't one ruin — it's a 18-site archaeological region spread across roughly a 10 km radius, layering four separate cities (Saraikala, Bhir Mound, Sirkap, Sirsukh) on top of each other like sediment, plus the Buddhist monastery-and-stupa complexes at Dharmarajika, Jaulian, and Mohra Moradu.

Timeline

From Bharata's legend to the bulldozer of the 20th century

  • c. 1000 BCE

    First major settlement at Hathial mound; early trade links with the wider Gandhara region.

  • 6th c. BCE

    Gandhara, with Taxila as a key city, is absorbed into the Achaemenid Persian Empire; Herodotus later calls it one of the empire's wealthiest territories.

  • 327 BCE

    Alexander the Great reaches Taxila on his campaign into the subcontinent.

  • 3rd c. BCE

    Mauryan rule; the city becomes a renowned centre of learning, traditionally linked to scholars such as Chanakya (Kautilya).

  • 2nd c. BCE – 1st c. CE

    Indo-Greek city of Sirkap is laid out on a Hellenistic grid plan — home to the famous Double-Headed Eagle Stupa, fusing Greek, Buddhist and local motifs.

  • 1st–2nd c. CE

    Kushan Empire; Sirsukh founded by Kanishka. This is the golden age of Gandharan Buddhist art produced at Jaulian, Mohra Moradu and Dharmarajika.

  • 5th c. CE

    Hun invasions destroy the city; trade routes shift and Taxila fades from history for over a thousand years.

  • 1863–1873

    Sir Alexander Cunningham rediscovers and identifies the ruins for the Archaeological Survey of India.

  • 1913–1934

    Sir John Marshall conducts the major systematic excavations that reveal Sirkap, Sirsukh, and the monastery complexes.

  • 1980

    Taxila is inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List.

  • March 2026

    A visitor alerts Pakistan's Permanent Delegate to UNESCO in Paris to fresh cement masonry replacing original stonework at two sites.

  • July 2026

    UNESCO formally warns Pakistan to reverse the work or risk Taxila being placed on the List of World Heritage in Danger.

Why it matters right now

This isn't a history problem. It's happening this month.

In March 2026, photos submitted to UNESCO showed original, irregular ancient stonework at Mohra Moradu (a Kushan-era Buddhist monastery) and Sirkap (the Indo-Greek city layer) replaced with fresh, uniform modern masonry and cement — and, in places, walls rebuilt taller than they were. UNESCO called these "unnecessary interventions" that damage the site's authenticity, and told Pakistani officials it would not hesitate to act, citing its earlier delisting of a World Heritage Site in Germany for the same reason.

The stakes

Punjab's Archaeology Department disputes the framing, saying the work was structural stabilisation, not rebuilding — but the underlying tension is the one this whole project is about: what does it actually mean to "preserve" a place like this, and who gets to decide? A site that survived a thousand years of empires can still lose its status over a decade of well-intentioned concrete.

Who this is for

Audience & demographic context

Local day-trippers

Families and students from Islamabad/Rawalpindi — Taxila is a ~45-minute drive via the Grand Trunk Road, making it Pakistan's most accessible major heritage site.

Regional & diaspora Buddhists

Pilgrims from Sri Lanka, Southeast Asia, Korea and Japan, for whom Gandharan Taxila is a foundational site in the spread of Buddhist art and doctrine.

Heritage & conservation students

The current UNESCO dispute makes Taxila a live case study in conservation ethics — exactly the audience this page and presentation are aimed at.
